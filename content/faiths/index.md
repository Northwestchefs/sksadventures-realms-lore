# Faiths

The divine life of the Realms in **1490 DR** is active, local, and rarely cleanly separated into "one true church" lines. Most communities keep multiple shrines, most households honor multiple gods, and most rulers treat religion as both spiritual reality and civic infrastructure.

Use this section as a **table-ready reference** for post-Second Sundering play: who is publicly strong, where faith institutions matter most, and what tensions are most likely to generate adventures.

## Forgotten Realms Religion in 1490 DR (At a Glance)

- **Polytheism is the norm:** people pray by need (voyage, harvest, trial, childbirth, battle), not strict exclusive membership.
- **Temples are service institutions:** healing, burial, mediation, famine relief, warding rites, records, and legal witnessing.
- **The gods are recent in living memory:** older generations remember upheaval eras (avatars walking, magical collapse, and later divine realignments).
- **Doctrine varies by city and clergy:** two temples of the same god can diverge in tone, politics, and local allies.
- **Statecraft and religion interlock:** rulers rarely ignore major temple networks, especially in trade hubs, ports, and border fortresses.

## Era Context: Why 1490 DR Feels Different

### Time of Troubles Legacy (1358 DR)

- The era when gods walked Faerûn still shapes sermons, relic traditions, and cautionary theology.
- Many priesthoods frame this period as proof that divine order can fracture when pride, overreach, or cosmic crisis strikes.

### Spellplague and Century of Upheaval (from 1385 DR)

- Magical catastrophe, state collapse, and doctrinal shock reshaped temple influence unevenly across regions.
- Some priesthoods lost local authority; others filled governance vacuums with charity, militias, and civic rituals.

### Second Sundering Aftermath (late 15th century DR)

- By 1490 DR, many communities treat this as a re-balancing era: old cults re-emerge, reformed churches stabilize, and local compromises harden into new norms.
- In campaign terms: faith politics are **recovered but not settled**.

## Major Faith Currents in Late-15th-Century Play

### Civic Order and Justice

- **Tyr, Torm, Ilmater, Helm** traditions often anchor courts, garrisons, oaths, and public trust.
- Their clergy commonly arbitrate between rulers, guilds, mercenary captains, and commoners during crisis.

### Trade, Coin, and Urban Stability

- **Waukeen** and allied mercantile cults influence caravans, contracts, harbor law, and debt morality.
- In wealthy cities, temple-backed credit and arbitration can matter as much as noble decree.

### Magic and Arcane Legitimacy

- **Mystra's** worship regained strategic relevance wherever wards, portals, academy politics, and magical disaster response are civic concerns.
- Arcane faith institutions often compete and cooperate with secular mage orders.

### War, Borders, and Survival

- **Tempus** and martial cults surge where frontier war is chronic.
- In hard-border regions, clergy can function as morale officers, quartermasters, and truce brokers.

### Death, Night, and Fear Economies

- **Kelemvor, Myrkul, Bhaal, Shar, Selûne** currents often collide in burial practice, urban crime anxieties, moonlit rites, and secret-society politics.
- Most cities maintain a public-facing death doctrine while quietly tolerating older local customs.

## How Religion Usually Works at Table Level

### Household Practice

- Families keep small icon sets and rotate prayers by season and need.
- "Primary deity" identity is common among clergy, paladins, and zealots; less so among ordinary folk.

### Temple Economics

- Offerings fund poor relief, funerals, healers, and protective rites.
- Rich temples become lenders, landholders, and political actors.

### Local Variation

- A dockside shrine culture may be practical and transactional.
- A court temple may be ceremonial and legalistic.
- A frontier chapel may be militant and communal.

## Campaign-Ready Tension Patterns

- **Temple vs. guild law:** who controls contracts, loans, and harbor disputes?
- **Orthodoxy vs. local custom:** whose rite is "proper" after a crisis season?
- **Public shrine vs. hidden cult:** tolerated secrets become emergency threats.
- **Relic legitimacy:** rival claims over a relic can redraw political alliances.
- **Divine service overload:** one plague, siege, or storm can shatter a city's faith equilibrium.
